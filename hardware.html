Meta Quest (passthrough MR) Standalone headset with built-in hand tracking and passthrough video, so the real room and virtual content blend without a phone in hand. One-time hardware cost, no subscription for the device itself Medium to high, usually via Unity or native SDKs; WebXR-in-headset-browser is a lighter option App store (Meta Horizon Store) or sideload Yes, once installed Medium. WebXR-in-headset stays vibecodable; the native SDK path inherits the same constraints as the native and engine category. Not yet used
Apple Vision Pro visionOS headset, RealityKit and SwiftUI, the highest passthrough fidelity available right now. Expensive hardware, plus a Mac and Apple Developer account for development High App Store or ad-hoc install Yes, once installed Medium, same profile as ARKit in the native and engine category, with a much smaller installed base to reach. Not yet used
Android XR Google's XR platform (headset hardware from partners such as Samsung) combining passthrough mixed reality with on-device AI assistance, positioned as a newer competitor to Quest and Vision Pro. Hardware cost varies by manufacturer Medium to high, via Android XR's SDK, closer in spirit to ARCore than to Unity/Unreal Google Play, or sideload Yes, once installed Medium, similar profile to ARCore development generally. Not yet used
Magic Leap 2 An enterprise-focused optical see-through headset (a genuinely transparent display, not video passthrough), still actively sold into field service, medical, and industrial use cases where HoloLens has pulled back. Expensive, enterprise-oriented High Enterprise deployment tools, not a consumer app store Yes, once installed Low. Niche tooling and a narrow, enterprise-oriented ecosystem. Not yet used
HoloLens 2 Also an enterprise-focused optical see-through headset. Microsoft has deprioritized new hardware here, so treat this row as legacy rather than a current recommendation. Expensive, enterprise-oriented High Enterprise deployment tools, not a consumer app store Yes, once installed Low. Niche tooling and a shrinking ecosystem. Not yet used
Smart glasses, camera-only Devices like Ray-Ban Meta glasses: camera, audio, and on-device AI, with no AR visual display at all. Useful for capture and voice interaction, not for showing virtual content. Consumer hardware pricing Low, mostly a companion app, no AR display to build for Platform-locked companion app No Low. There is no AR display surface to develop for. Not yet used
Smart glasses, with display Devices like Snap Spectacles (developer program) and Meta Ray-Ban Display glasses: a real, if limited, in-lens display, distinct from the camera-only glasses above. Developer kits and consumer units vary widely, generally expensive relative to a phone Depends on the device; Spectacles inherits Lens Studio's curve Platform-locked in both cases No Low. Same constraint as the no-code platforms these devices are tied to. Not yet used
None of the projects behind this atlas run on dedicated hardware yet. Everything so far targets a handheld phone browser, by design: it is the option with zero install friction for gallery visitors and workshop participants. A headset would only make sense for a piece that specifically needs both hands free for an extended, multi-visitor gallery run.
